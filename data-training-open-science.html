1. About Open Science

Science does not end with publication

Science has always depended on what could be carried forward.

Today, we produce more data, more code, and more results than at any point in history. Yet much of it cannot be accessed, understood, or reused. It remains stored, but not transmitted. Preserved, but rarely usable. Studies estimate that a large proportion of research data are never shared or are lost over time, with some analyses suggesting that up to 80% of data become unavailable within two decades [1]. At the same time, concerns about reproducibility persist across disciplines, with surveys indicating that more than 70% of researchers have failed to reproduce another scientist’s experiments, and over half have failed to reproduce their own [2].

The problem is not always the absence of knowledge. It is the failure to make it continue.

Open science emerges in this context, not as a new ideal, but as a response to an old condition. Knowledge only exists within a system that allows it to be taken further.

What openness really means

Open science is often reduced to access. But access alone is insufficient.

As articulated in The Turing Way, openness is inseparable from transparency and reproducibility, emphasizing that research must be understandable and repeatable in order to be useful [3]. The materials developed through FOSTER Open Science Training Toolkit further stress that what matters is not simply whether research is visible, but whether it can be understood and reused within and across disciplines [4].

A dataset that is technically available but undocumented does not contribute to knowledge. A method that cannot be reproduced does not extend knowledge. In both cases, something has been produced, but it cannot be carried forward. This aligns with broader principles of research reproducibility and reuse, which highlight that accessibility without interpretability fails to support cumulative science [5].

But openness does not begin at publication. It begins much earlier, in how research is designed, documented, and managed. Decisions made at the start of a project, how data are structured, how methods are recorded, how files are organized, already determine whether that work will remain usable. Reproducibility, often treated as a technical requirement, is in practice a question of continuity. Could someone else, or even your future self, understand what was done?

When continuity fails

Modern science is cumulative, but its continuity is fragile.

Data are stored without context. Code depends on environments that are never recorded. Methods are described incompletely. Results cannot be reproduced. These are not marginal issues. They define whether knowledge remains usable.

When this happens, knowledge does not disappear immediately. It becomes unusable. It cannot be verified, it cannot be extended, and it cannot enter into the broader system of cumulative knowledge.

This is not simply a matter of efficiency. It is where continuity fails.

The difficulty is not always resistance. It is often structural. Researchers work under constraints of time, incentives, and evaluation systems that do not always reward careful documentation or sharing. For this reason, the gap between principle and practice persists.

What it means to make knowledge usable: The FAIR Principles

To make knowledge continue requires deliberate practice.

It begins early, in how data are organized, described, and stored. It extends to how methods are documented, how code is written and shared, and how results are communicated. It includes where research outputs are deposited, whether they remain accessible, and whether they can be interpreted without the presence of their author.

This requirement is formalized in what are known as the FAIR Principles, as a guide on how research data should be managed and shared. FAIR does not simply mean that data are open. It defines the conditions under which data can be found, accessed, understood, and reused.

Data should be findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable [5]. These principles shift the focus from visibility to functionality. Data are not considered valuable simply because they exist or are available, but because they can be meaningfully used by others, including those outside the original research context.

What is critical is that FAIR does not require all data to be openly available. It requires that data be as open as possible and as closed as necessary, while remaining usable under defined conditions [5].

In this sense, FAIR operationalizes a fundamental principle of science. Knowledge must not only be preserved. It must remain interpretable, verifiable, and extendable.

Without these conditions, data may exist, but they do not contribute to cumulative knowledge.

Responsibility, not additional work

Open science is often perceived as an added burden. In practice, it is part of doing science.

To contribute to knowledge is to assume responsibility for its transmission.

This responsibility is not abstract. Knowledge can only be transmitted if it remains interpretable, accessible, and reusable. Data that are undocumented, inaccessible, or irreproducible break this chain. In such cases, knowledge is not immediately lost, but it becomes unusable, and therefore cannot be built upon.

Preservation alone is not sufficient. What must be preserved is the ability to understand, verify, and extend what was produced.

Open science does not require a complete transformation. It often begins with small decisions: documenting a dataset, structuring files clearly, sharing code, choosing a repository. These actions, taken consistently, determine whether knowledge can be reused.
